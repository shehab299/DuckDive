150 Years of Impressionism

April 15, 1874, was a landmark day in art, marking the opening of the first exhibition by a group who would later become known as Impressionists. And it may be easy to forget, when looking at their paintings of middle-class leisure, but the Impressionists were radicals. They broke from academic tradition, painting modern life rather than historical or mythological subjects, and were more concerned with capturing ephemeral moments than using illusionist tricks. As Impressionism turns 150, learn more about the artists and work that defined an era.

Impressionist or Not?

The Impressionist collective included Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Berthe Morisot, Edgar Degas, and, strictly speaking, any artist who exhibited work in one of the eight Impressionist exhibitions—from Paul Cézanne to Paul Gauguin to Georges Seurat. Many other contemporary artists both influenced and were influenced by the movement—but never participated in the exhibitions. Here are four such artists commonly mistaken for Impressionists.
Édouard Manet
The work of French artist Édouard Manet in the 1860s greatly influenced the group of younger artists who would become the original Impressionists, and by 1873 Manet’s friendship with the group led to his own experimentation with their approach. Yet Manet maintained his realist style for most of his career and never participated in the Impressionist exhibitions.
Auguste Rodin
A contemporary of the Impressionist group, the French sculptor Auguste Rodin was friendly with its members, especially Monet. His work also shows similar themes to those of the movement, including an interest in the effect of light and emphasis on materiality, imperfect surfaces, and subjects depicted without allegory or embellishment. Rodin, however, never showed his work in the Impressionist exhibitions.
Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec
Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec is perhaps best known for documenting with great psychological insight the personalities and facets of Parisian nightlife and the French world of entertainment in the 1890s. He settled in Paris in 1882, the year of the seventh Impressionist exhibition. Toulouse-Lautrec, however, never formally joined any theoretical school and instead readily lifted techniques and styles from all of art history to achieve a particular effect.
Vincent van Gogh
Vincent van Gogh arrived in Paris in 1886, in time to catch the eighth and final Impressionist exhibition. His eventual friendships with a number of the participants, including Camille Pissaro, Georges Seurat and Paul Gaugin, led to changes in his painting which resembled the work of the Impressionists. His palette is more colorful, his vision less traditional, and his brushwork more broken. By 1888, his own unique Post-Impressionist style had crystallized.
